A Practical, Example-Driven Guide

I recently wrote my thesis in LaTeX. Not as a “power user” flex, but because I needed a tool that wouldn’t collapse under 300+ pages of chapters, equations, tables, cross-references, appendices, and constantly changing reviewer comments.

Three lessons stood out: (1) treat structure as data, not decoration; (2) let references and numbering be computed, not hand-edited; and (3) build a reproducible pipeline so formatting never becomes a bottleneck. Below is exactly how I set this up.

1 — A project skeleton that won’t fall apart at 2 a.m.

Break the document into modules. One file per chapter, a clean preamble, and a single “conductor” file.

thesis/
├─ main.tex
├─ preamble/
│  └─ packages.tex
├─ chapters/
│  ├─ 01-introduction.tex
│  ├─ 02-literature.tex
│  ├─ 03-methods.tex
│  ├─ 04-results.tex
│  └─ 05-discussion.tex
├─ figures/
│  ├─ conceptual-model.pdf
│  └─ rwi-trends.png
├─ tables/
│  └─ descriptives.csv
├─ bib/
│  └─ references.bib
└─ build/

main.tex (top-level orchestration):

\documentclass[12pt,oneside]{report} % or your uni's thesis class
\input{preamble/packages}

\addbibresource{bib/references.bib}
\graphicspath

\begin{document}
\frontmatter
\pagenumbering{roman}
\tableofcontents
\listoffigures
\listoftables

\mainmatter
\pagenumbering{arabic}
\include{chapters/01-introduction}
\include{chapters/02-literature}
\include{chapters/03-methods}
\include{chapters/04-results}
\include{chapters/05-discussion}

\appendix
\include{chapters/A1-appendix}

\printbibliography
\end{document}

preamble/packages.tex (all setup in one place):

% Use LuaLaTeX or XeLaTeX for modern fonts
\usepackage{fontspec}
\usepackage{microtype}
\usepackage[a4paper,margin=30mm]{geometry}
\usepackage{setspace}\onehalfspacing
\usepackage{csquotes}
\usepackage[backend=biber,style=apa]{biblatex}
\usepackage{hyperref}                % load before cleveref
\usepackage[capitalise,noabbrev]{cleveref}
\usepackage{graphicx}
\usepackage{subcaption}
\usepackage{booktabs}
\usepackage{siunitx}
\usepackage{amsmath,amssymb,amsthm}
\usepackage{enumitem}
\usepackage{placeins}                % \FloatBarrier

This separation keeps chapters about content, and not about package wrangling.

2 — Cross-references that cannot break (even when you reorganize)

LaTeX’s labels and references mean you never type “Figure 3.2” again.

\section{Conceptual Model}
\label{sec:conceptual-model}

As shown in \cref{fig:model}, liminal spaces mediate the effect of
remote work intensity on well-being.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.85\textwidth]{conceptual-model.pdf}
  \caption{Conceptual model: remote work intensity → liminal spaces → outcomes.}
  \label{fig:model}
\end{figure}

Reorder figures, insert new sections — \cref{fig:model} always resolves to the correct, updated number and prefix (“Figure”, “Table”, “Section”).

3 — Citations with biblatex (one source of truth)

I kept all references in bib/references.bib and never touched formatting again.

@article{doe2020,
  author  = {Doe, Alex and Smith, Jordan},
  title   = {Liminal Spaces and Remote Work Intensity},
  journal = {Journal of Work Studies},
  year    = {2020},
  volume  = {15},
  number  = {3},
  pages   = {211--235},
  doi     = {10.0000/example.doi}
}

In text:

\textcite{doe2020} argue that structured transitions reduce technostress,
while other studies report benefits at moderate remote-work levels \parencite{doe2020}.

If a journal demanded another style, I changed style=apa to style=authoryear, recompiled, and moved on.

Compile reliably (with Biber):

latexmk -lualatex -use-biber main.tex

4 — Tables that look like they came from a journal

booktabs for rules; siunitx for alignment and consistent number formatting.

\begin{table}[!htb]
  \centering
  \caption{Descriptive statistics by remote work intensity (RWI)}
  \label{tab:descriptives}
  \sisetup{table-format=2.2}
  \begin{tabular}{l S S S}
    \toprule
    Group          & {Well-being} & {Technostress} & {Creativity} \\
    \midrule
    Low RWI        & 5.12         & 2.90           & 4.20 \\
    Moderate RWI   & 5.45         & 2.30           & 4.75 \\
    High RWI       & 4.90         & 3.40           & 4.10 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Editors notice this polish. More importantly, you never fight spacing hacks.

5 — Figures and float placement that behave

I used [!htb] to guide placement and \FloatBarrier before major breaks to keep figures from drifting too far.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.7\textwidth]{rwi-trends.png}
  \caption{Weekly remote work intensity (illustrative).}
  \label{fig:rwi-trends}
\end{figure}

\FloatBarrier

Whenever possible, I exported plots as PDF for crisp vector output.

6 — Equations, alignment, and reusable notation

Even if your thesis isn’t math-heavy, amsmath makes aligned models and references painless.

\begin{align}
  \text{Creativity}_i &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{RWI}_i + \beta_2 \text{LS}_i + \epsilon_i, \label{eq:creativity} \\
  \text{WellBeing}_i &= \gamma_0 + \gamma_1 \text{RWI}_i + \gamma_2 \text{LS}_i + \eta_i. \label{eq:wellbeing}
\end{align}

Then refer with \cref{eq:creativity}. No manual numbering, ever.

Define environments where needed:

\newtheorem{definition}{Definition}
\begin{definition}
Liminal spaces are transitional zones that support psychological shifts
between work and personal roles.
\end{definition}

7 — Appendices, acronyms, glossary, and index (no last-minute chaos)

Appendix numbering is automatic:

\appendix
\chapter{Survey Instrument}
\label{app:instrument}

For acronyms/terms:

% preamble
\usepackage[acronym]{glossaries}
\makeglossaries
\newacronym{rwi}{RWI}{Remote Work Intensity}

% in text
\gls{rwi} influences daily routines.

% near the end
\printglossaries

If your committee likes indices:

\usepackage{imakeidx}
\makeindex
Liminal spaces\index{liminal spaces} mediate transitions...
\printindex

8 — Collaboration that scales: Overleaf, Git, and diffs

  • Overleaf handled supervisor comments and tracked changes.
  • Git gave me version history and safe branching before risky edits.
  • latexdiff produced human-readable change PDFs for resubmissions:
latexdiff old.tex new.tex > diff.tex
lualatex diff.tex
  • Reproducible pipeline: I generated figures/tables from R/Python, saved into figures/ and tables/, and latexmk rebuilt the PDF end-to-end. One command, no manual glue.

9) Accessibility, navigation, and typographic polish

  • hyperref added PDF bookmarks and clickable references—examiners used them heavily.
  • microtype improved kerning/hyphenation quietly.
  • Avoid widows/orphans:
\clubpenalty=10000
\widowpenalty=10000

If your library mandates PDF/A, check requirements early — LaTeX can do it, but don’t discover that the night before submission.

10 — Common failure points (and fast fixes)

  • Undefined citations/refs: you likely forgot to run Biber, or ran it in the wrong order. Use latexmk -lualatex -use-biber main.tex.
  • Missing packages: install a full TeX Live/MiKTeX; on Overleaf, just add the package in the preamble and recompile.
  • Float chaos: don’t stack back-to-back figures/tables; give them text context and add \FloatBarrier before new sections.
  • Font confusion: fontspec requires LuaLaTeX/XeLaTeX. If you switch to pdfLaTeX, remove fontspec and use \usepackage[T1]{fontenc}\usepackage[utf8]{inputenc} instead.
  • Copy-pasted Word tables: rebuild with booktabs/siunitx—it’s faster than debugging broken spacing.

11 — Templates and compliance

If your university provides a thesis class or template, start there. It bakes in margins, title pages, numbering depth, and page order. If not, lock down:

  • page size/margins (geometry)
  • line spacing (setspace)
  • TOC depth (\setcounter{tocdepth}{...})
  • front matter order (abstract, acknowledgments, declaration)
  • bibliography style (biblatex style chosen with your department)

12 — When someone insists on Word

You can export to .docx with Pandoc (expect to tidy complex floats/tables):

pandoc main.tex \
  --from=latex \
  --to=docx \
  --citeproc \
  --resource-path=.:figures:tables \
  --output=thesis.docx

For anything final, keep the LaTeX-produced PDF as the authoritative version.

If you want a lean, sane default:

Why I wouldn’t write a thesis any other way

My structure changed dozens of times. New hypotheses, research questions, relocated figures, equations, reworked appendices.

With LaTeX, nothing “snapped.” Section numbers, cross-references, lists of figures/tables, and citations stayed coherent because LaTeX computed them for me. The typography was clean by default. Review cycles were faster because I wasn’t firefighting formatting.

If you adopt just three practices, modular chapters, biblatex for citations, and booktabs/siunitx for tables, you’ll feel the same stability. Add sensible float management, latexmk automation, and Overleaf/Git for collaboration, and you have a workflow that lets you focus on argument and evidence, not on fighting your document. That’s why I chose LaTeX, and why, after finishing my thesis, I wouldn’t use anything else.
